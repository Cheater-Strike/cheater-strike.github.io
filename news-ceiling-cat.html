Valve Hires Ceiling Cat in Desperate Bid to Eradicate CS2 Cheating

Cheater Strike Newsdesk · 18 July 2026

BELLEVUE, WA — Following months of community outrage over Premier lobbies degenerating into 10-man spinbot recreation centers, Valve has officially announced the decommissioning of VACNet 3.0. In its place, the publisher has tapped a 2006 internet deity to secure the servers: Ceiling Cat.

"We realized that server-side AI was fundamentally flawed," explained a Valve spokesperson while actively designing a new Fruits & Vegetables sticker capsule. "Cheaters figured out how to spoof their view angles to VAC Live. The AI thought everything was fine while a guy named 'xX_G0D_A1M_Xx' was doing 360s at 10,000 RPM with an auto-sniper. So, we needed an anti-cheat that bypassed the code entirely. We needed someone who could physically look down upon the sinners."

According to the leaked patch notes, the new system—officially dubbed Feline Anti-Cheat (FAC)—eschews traditional kernel-level access in favor of streaming players' webcam feeds directly to a centralized server farm staffed entirely by aloof, judgmental domestic shorthairs perched on suspended acoustic tiles.

"We found that while sophisticated AI struggles to differentiate between a 400-millisecond flick-shot and a synthetic aimbot, a nine-year-old tabby knows intrinsically when a human is behaving without honor," the patch notes read. "FAC doesn't scan your hard drive. It simply watches you track an opponent through a solid wall on Dust II, squints at your lack of integrity, and silently judges your life choices."

Early beta testers of FAC report a chilling shift in the matchmaking experience. Rather than issuing instant hardware bans, the system relies on deep, passive-aggressive psychological warfare. Players flagged by the felines report experiencing profound, creeping feelings of inadequacy and a sudden urge to apologize to their parents.

"I just downloaded a simple wallhack to boost my Elo," admitted one forum user on a prominent cheat provider website. "But every time I toggled it on, a small pop-up appeared in the corner of my screen showing an orange cat staring at me with a mixture of pity and disgust. I uninstalled the cheat, deleted the game, and I'm looking into community college."

Despite these early successes, cybersecurity analysts warn the battle is far from over. Premium cheat developers have reportedly abandoned code injection entirely, instead offering a $20-a-month digital laser pointer overlay designed to temporarily distract the Valve felines during crucial clutch rounds.

When asked how the company plans to combat these low-tech workarounds, Valve leadership remained characteristically indifferent.

"We understand that maintaining competitive integrity is an ongoing process, and we are working to incrementally improve FAC's attention span," Valve stated in a brief press release. "In the meantime, to celebrate this new era of security, players can purchase the new 'Empty Cardboard Box' weapon charm, available now in the Armory for $14.99."
